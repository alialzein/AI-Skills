Strong Team Leadership · Lesson 2 of your course

Make it safe to tell you the truth

The biggest risk to a team isn't bad news — it's bad news you never hear. Your job is to make truth-telling safe, so problems reach you while they're still small.

Why this, first

Google studied 180+ teams (Project Aristotle) and found the #1 driver of high performance was psychological safety: the shared belief you won't be punished for speaking up, admitting a mistake, or disagreeing. [re:Work] The concept comes from Harvard's Amy Edmondson, who showed that the best teams don't make fewer mistakes — they're just safe enough to report them. [HBS]

You lead through people. People only surface problems early when the cost of speaking up is lower than the cost of staying quiet — and you set that cost.

So the foundational skill isn't fixing problems faster. It's making your team willing to bring them to you while they're still cheap to fix.

The tool: make it safe in three moves

Edmondson distilled it into three things a leader does, over and over. Skip them and silence fills the gap.

  1. Frame the work as a learning problem, not a test. ("We've never done this before — I'll need everyone's input to get it right.") Lowers the cost of speaking up.
  2. Invite participation by asking real questions and modelling your own fallibility. ("What am I missing?" / "Who sees this differently?") Admit when you don't know.
  3. Respond productively — especially to bad news. Thank the messenger, get curious, never punish honesty. One punished truth-teller silences the whole room.

None of this lowers your standards. It raises candor — which is what keeps standards real instead of theatrical.

See the difference

A report tells you: "We're going to miss the deadline."

✗ Destroys safety

"Why am I only hearing this now?! This is on you."

Result: next time, they hide it until it explodes — and you lose the one warning you actually needed.

✓ Builds safety

"Thank you for flagging it early — that's exactly what I need. What's driving the slip, and what would help?"

Result: the message and the messenger both come back next time, sooner.
