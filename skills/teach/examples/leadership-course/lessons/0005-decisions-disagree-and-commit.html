Strong Team Leadership · Lesson 5 of your course

Decide fast, commit together

Weak leadership often looks like indecision — endless debate, or decisions that quietly reverse a week later. Strong leaders make the call at the right speed and get everyone rowing, even those who disagreed.

Why this, first

If you tend to over-deliberate in search of consensus — or let calls drift until they make themselves — your team stalls waiting on you. Jeff Bezos built Amazon's decision framework on exactly this problem: separating the decisions that deserve caution from the ones that just need someone to decide. [2016 shareholder letter] It's a core piece of what Amazon calls staying "Day 1" — keeping the speed of a small company even as you grow. [Day 1 culture]

Speed matters in business. Most decisions should be made with somewhere around 70% of the information you wish you had. If you wait for 90%, you're probably being too slow.

So the skill is calibrating: decide the reversible things fast, reserve the slow care for the few that truly can't be undone — and then bring everyone with you.

The tool: three questions before any decision

Run these in order before you make — or stall on — any call that matters.

  1. One-way or two-way door? — Is this reversible? Two-way-door (reversible) decisions should be made fast, by someone close to the work. One-way-door (hard to undo) decisions deserve slow, careful analysis. Most decisions are two-way doors treated as if they were one-way — that's what slows teams down.
  2. Who decides? — Name the single decider up front. "Everyone and no one decides" is how decisions rot. Input from many; decision by one.
  3. Do we have ~70%? — Make most decisions with about 70% of the information you wish you had. Wait for 90% and you're too slow; you can course-correct a good two-way-door call.

Then close it: disagree and commit. Once the decider calls it, everyone commits fully — including those who argued against it. Name it out loud: "We disagree, but will you commit with me on this?"

See the difference

✗ Stuck

The team has debated a project-management tool for three weeks. You keep seeking consensus, and nobody owns the call.

No decider, no deadline, no door. The debate just keeps regenerating itself.

✓ Strong

"This is a two-way door — we can switch later. I'll decide by Friday with what we know. If you disagree, say so now; after Friday we all commit."
